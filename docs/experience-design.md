# Experience Design

## Desired Feeling
Relevance Agent should feel calm, supportive, and useful in a real moment.

The user may arrive overwhelmed, avoidant, scattered, ashamed, tired, or emotionally activated. The interface should reduce the load, not add another system to manage.

The app should feel:
- Calm
- Emotionally safe
- Practical
- Lightweight
- Encouraging
- Personal without feeling invasive
- Motivating without pressure
- Reflective without becoming heavy
- Delightful without becoming noisy

It should not feel:
- Busy
- Judgy
- Gamified in a shallow way
- Like a generic chatbot demo
- Like a productivity app yelling at the user
- Like a therapist replacement
- Emotionally flat in the name of simplicity

## Interaction Principles

### Low Friction
The main loop should be easy to understand:

1. Name the mission.
2. Choose the mentor mode.
3. Describe what is happening.
4. Get guidance.
5. Complete one next step.
6. See progress.

Avoid extra decisions unless they clearly help the user.

### Small Steps
The app should make action feel smaller, not larger.

Good interaction patterns:
- One mission input.
- Four clear mentor modes.
- Prompt chips for quick starts.
- One response.
- One helpful phrase.
- One next step.
- One completion button.

Avoid:
- Long forms.
- Too many settings.
- Multiple competing next actions.
- Dense dashboards.
- Abstract progress that does not connect to the user's action.

### Immediate Feedback
When the user completes a step, the app should respond quickly and visibly.

Immediate feedback can include:
- Token progress increases.
- Completed step count increases.
- Step log updates.
- Quote or reinforcement copy changes.
- Rocket progress communicates movement.

Feedback should feel satisfying, but not so loud that it overwhelms the mentor experience.

## Progress Philosophy

### Tokens
Tokens represent completed next steps. They are not a measure of worth, discipline, or productivity.

Token feedback should communicate:
- "You moved."
- "That counted."
- "Small steps matter."
- "Momentum can be built gently."

Avoid making tokens feel like:
- A punishment system.
- A scoreboard against other people.
- A reason to shame missed days.
- The only thing that matters.

In rocket mode, tokens also act as countdown fuel. The user should feel that each completed step helps prepare the launch.

### Milestones
Milestones should make progress feel meaningful without turning the app into a noisy game.

Milestones should:
- Reinforce consistency.
- Create a feeling of earned movement.
- Connect to the rocket/progress metaphor.
- Celebrate completion without demanding perfection.

Milestones should not:
- Interrupt the user's emotional safety.
- Create pressure to keep clicking.
- Become more important than the mentor guidance.

Milestone design should preserve hooks for:
- quote or reflective framing
- sound feedback
- animation feedback
- themed journey moments
- future major milestone sequences

For the rocket theme, major milestones should eventually support a launch / travel / arrival arc. The long-term emotional direction is Earth to Mars, where Mars represents transformation and a larger vision-board outcome.

### Reinforcement
Reinforcement copy should be short, warm, and believable.

Good reinforcement:
- "Good. One step counts."
- "Fuel collected. Ready for launch."
- "Momentum begins before confidence."

Bad reinforcement:
- "You crushed it, superstar, now dominate the day."
- "Do not stop now or you will lose progress."
- "Only ten more steps until you are successful."

## Calm Visual Direction
Visual design should support regulation and clarity.

Use:
- Clear spacing.
- High readability.
- Strong hierarchy.
- Soft contrast where possible.
- Motion only when it helps feedback.
- Warm accents for action.
- Green or fuel-like progress cues for momentum.

Avoid:
- Dense panels.
- Constant motion.
- Excessive alerts.
- Aggressive red warning states.
- Decorative effects that compete with the mentor response.

Motion and sound should be used as meaningful reward feedback, not decoration. When simplifying the UI, do not remove quote, milestone, animation, or sound hooks without explicitly noting the tradeoff.

## Multi-Page Direction
The MVP can remain one page while the product direction moves toward a clearer multi-page mentor ecosystem.

Future page areas:
- Mentor Home: constant guide, today's focus, next best step, grounding quote.
- Tasks / Priorities: task capture, prioritization, due dates, consequence-aware work.
- Calendar / Schedule: schedule awareness and future calendar integration.
- Regulation / Support: panic, overwhelm, and relationship support.
- Milestones / Journey: celebration, reward loops, and themed milestone experiences.

The mentor should feel consistent across these pages. Each page should reduce cognitive load by giving one clear purpose instead of crowding every feature into one screen.

## Daily Check-In Experience
The daily check-in should stay small and optional.

It should help the app understand:
- How the user is arriving.
- What kind of support may help.
- What matters today.

It should not become:
- A long mood tracker.
- A clinical assessment.
- A required form before getting help.
- A separate product surface too early.

## Partner/Parent Sensitivity Rules
Partner/Parent mode must be handled with extra care because users may be in conflict, shame, fear, or family stress.

The experience should:
- Slow the user down.
- Protect both people.
- Avoid blame.
- Offer short safe phrases.
- Encourage pauses when activation is high.
- Support reconnection without forcing it.
- Keep parenting language firm, gentle, and non-shaming.

The experience should not:
- Pick sides.
- Diagnose the other person.
- Encourage escalation.
- Encourage long arguments by text.
- Treat conflict like a debate to win.
- Push repair when space is safer.
- Ignore signs of danger or coercion.

Good partner-mode interaction:
- User says they are both triggered.
- App gives a short calming frame.
- App offers one sentence they can say.
- App encourages a pause if needed.

Bad partner-mode interaction:
- User says they are fighting.
- App analyzes the entire relationship.
- App assigns blame.
- App suggests a long speech.

## Cognitive Load Rules
At every step, ask:
- Can the user understand what to do in five seconds?
- Is there only one primary action?
- Does this help someone who is tired or overwhelmed?
- Did we preserve the working mentor loop?
- Did we add complexity before it was needed?
- Did we preserve emotional resonance and motivating delight?
- Did we accidentally remove a quote, sound, animation, milestone, or theme hook?

If a design adds power but also adds confusion, defer it.

## Product Standard
The MVP does not need to feel finished.

It needs to feel real:
- A real person can arrive dysregulated.
- The app can help them settle.
- The app can help them choose one step.
- The app can record that progress.
- The app can make that step feel like it counted.
